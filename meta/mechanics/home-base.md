# Home Base

A party headquarters that starts small and grows through investment. Finding and establishing the base is part of early campaign arc — they don't start with one. Growth is tied to party choices and resources; mechanical benefits follow from investment.

## Overview

- **Encounter type:** crafting | exploration | social
- **Session fit:** long-term campaign arc; individual base-building sessions can be short or standard
- **Difficulty:** scalable
- **Player count:** any

## Design Intent

Humble beginnings matter — the base should start unglamorous and earn its upgrades. Players want a home they built, not one they were handed. Mechanical benefits (crafting stations, followers, storage, intel) should feel like earned infrastructure.

## Acquisition

Finding the base is an early campaign objective, not a starting condition. Could be:

- A ruined structure reclaimed
- A location gifted or won through an early mission
- A hidden site discovered through exploration
- A working establishment the party grows into — a place with an existing life that becomes theirs (see *A Life of Its Own*)

## Growth

Base expands through resource investment, faction relationships, and time. Each upgrade unlocks mechanical options:

- **Crafting stations** — enables weapon/item crafting (see [[crafting]])
- **Storage** — expanded inventory and resource banking
- **Followers / staff** — downtime tasks, intel gathering, base defense
- **Fortification** — the base can be threatened; investment makes it defensible

## A Life of Its Own

The base doesn't have to be a dead structure waiting for the party — it can be a place that was already alive before they arrived and stays alive around them. A tavern with regulars, an inn with travelers, a shop with customers, a ferry crossing, a bathhouse, a trading post: any establishment with its own business, staff, and rhythm. The party becomes part of it, then it becomes theirs — but it never stops being itself.

What this buys:

- **Built-in NPC traffic** — patrons, staff, and regulars walk through the party's home every day. Rumors, hooks, and recurring characters arrive on their own; no contrivance needed.
- **A second income and a second vulnerability** — the business earns, and the business can be threatened, taxed, boycotted, or burned. Enemies don't need to attack the party; they can attack the ledger.
- **Shared ownership of tone** — the establishment's existing identity (its name, its regulars, its reputation) merges with the party's instead of being authored by them from scratch. The home has history they didn't write, which makes the history they *do* write land harder.
- **Life during downtime** — the base generates scenes even when no one is basing. The barkeep has a problem, a regular went missing, a stranger took the corner table.

The establishment type should fit the party and the region — bar is the classic, but the principle is the living business, not the liquor.

## Identity

The base should feel like *theirs* — named, decorated, marked by their history. What they've hung on the walls, who they've let in, what they've lost there. Mechanical upgrades matter less than the fact that players would be angry if someone burned it down. Build toward that.

## DM Notes

- Base vulnerability is a hook, not a punishment — enemies knowing the base exists creates story
- Keep early upgrades achievable; players should feel progress within the first few arcs
- The base's location and character should reflect where the party started and who they are
