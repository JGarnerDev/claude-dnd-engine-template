# Clock System

Time pressure with two modes: a doom clock (bad things happen if the party moves too slowly) and opportunity windows (good things only available within a time limit). Party actions can move the clock in either direction — forward or back.

## Overview

- **Encounter type:** any — campaign-level framework, not a single encounter
- **Session fit:** any
- **Time estimate:** ambient pressure; clock state set at session start and updated at key moments
- **Difficulty:** scalable
- **Player count:** any

## Design Intent

Players wanted a world that genuinely reacts to their pace. Doing X before Y has real consequences. Urgency should feel earned, not artificial. Both threat and opportunity hang on timing.

## Modes

- **Doom clock** — a bad outcome is approaching. Delay accelerates it. Examples: villain completes a ritual, disease reaches a city, an army moves into position. Players can slow or stop the clock through targeted action.
- **Opportunity window** — a good outcome is only available for a limited time. Examples: a contact is in town only this week, a political faction is temporarily weakened and open to alliance, a rare resource is accessible before conditions change. Miss the window, the opportunity closes.

## Multiple Clocks

Multiple clocks run simultaneously. The party can't address all of them — choosing which to act on lets the others tick. This creates trolley problem situations: not "do we act" but "which consequence do we accept." Clocks should compete for the party's attention in ways that feel genuinely difficult, not obviously correct.

## What Moves the Clock

- Party choices at key decision points
- Missions completed or failed
- Time spent on side activities vs. main arc objectives
- Events triggered by other factions acting independently
- Choosing to address one clock always costs progress on another

## Visibility

- **Visible clocks** — party knows the stakes and the deadline. Urgency is explicit; they choose how to respond.
- **Hidden clocks** — players don't know the clock exists until signs accumulate or something triggers. Discovery is part of the tension.

Both are valid. Mix them across arcs — not every threat announces itself.

## DM Notes

- Track clock state explicitly — not as a hidden GM secret but as something the players have partial visibility into (rumors, observable signs)
- Clocks should never feel rigged; players should be able to understand what moved them after the fact
- Not every session needs an active clock — use for arcs with genuine stakes
