# Literary Devices & Twists

DM-craft palette for session planning. Not player-sourced desires (that's [[campaign-design-preferences]]) — these are structural tools for shaping how a session *tells* its story. Surface as light suggestions during planning; the DM picks or ignores. Suggest **at most one** per session, and only when the story state creates a natural opening. Never force a device into a hook that doesn't want one.

**Scale tags:** every entry carries a *Scale:* line with one or more of `scene | session | mission | arc | campaign` — the story spans the entry can structure. Multi-tagged entries work at any listed scale; the DM picks the scale per use, and the deployment note should say which was chosen. Scale determines where the suggestion fires and how much seeding runway it needs:

- `scene` / `session` — suggested during `/session` planning; little or no runway
- `mission` — suggested when a mission is being designed; the twist or device shapes the whole mission structure
- `arc` / `campaign` — suggested only at act transitions or campaign planning; needs seeds placed sessions or acts apart, so the decision to commit must come early

**Two lifecycles:**
- **Devices** are reusable. Track *Last used:* with a session wikilink so planning avoids repeating the same trick in back-to-back sessions. `—` means never used.
- **Twists** burn on use. Track *Deployed:* like [[campaign-design-preferences]] items, and *Seeded:* for breadcrumbs placed before the reveal. A deployed twist is retired — variants need to feel genuinely different, not reskinned.

**Seeding applies to devices too:** plant-and-pay-off devices (foreshadowing, Chekhov's gun, rule of three, bookending, parallel scenes) carry *Seeded:* lines while a payoff is pending — wikilink to the session plus a one-phrase note of what was planted and the intended payoff. A seeded device is *alive, not stalled*; it returns to normal reuse once the payoff lands (stamp *Last used:* and clear or annotate the seed). `/threads` surfaces seeded-but-unpaid items. **At act transitions, review every pending seed** — pay it off in the new act, re-seed it deliberately, or retire it consciously. Never let a planted gun rust silently.

**Twist safety check:** before suggesting any twist, sanity-check it against historian canon and `meta/mysteries.md`. A twist must not contradict established fact, and must not accidentally answer a load-bearing mystery the DM is saving. When a twist *intentionally* resolves a mystery, that's a DM decision to surface explicitly, never a side effect.

## Devices

Reusable narrative techniques. The note on each is the *table-level* application — what it looks like in actual play, not in prose.

- **Foreshadowing** — plant a detail this session that pays off 2+ sessions later. Works best when the detail is interesting on its own, so players don't smell setup.
  - *Scale: session, mission, arc* · *Last used: —*
- **Chekhov's gun** — an item, NPC ability, or location feature introduced casually that becomes load-bearing later. Differs from foreshadowing: it's a *tool* the party can pick up, not a hint.
  - *Scale: session, mission, arc* · *Last used: —*
- **Dramatic irony** — players (or one player) know something the characters don't, or the DM lets the table see a danger the party hasn't noticed. Cutaway scenes and overheard conversations are the delivery vehicles.
  - *Scale: scene, session* · *Last used: —*
- **In medias res** — open the session mid-action: combat already joined, the chase already running, the negotiation already going wrong. Backfill how they got there in the first lull.
  - *Scale: session* · *Last used: —*
- **Red herring** — a deliberately plantable false lead. Use sparingly and make the truth discoverable — a red herring with no path back to the real answer is just a wasted session.
  - *Scale: session, mission* · *Last used: —*
- **Ticking clock** — visible, concrete time pressure: the ritual completes at moonrise, the garrison arrives in three days. The clock must actually advance when players stall.
  - *Scale: scene, session, mission* · *Last used: —*
- **Parallel scenes** — two beats mirror each other: the party's choice echoes an NPC's earlier one, or two confrontations in one session rhyme. Players feel the pattern even when they can't name it.
  - *Scale: session, mission, arc* · *Last used: —*
- **Bookending** — the session (or arc) closes on an inverted image of how it opened: same place changed, same question with a new answer, same NPC on the other side of the table.
  - *Scale: session, mission, arc, campaign* · *Last used: —*
- **Cold open** — a scene before the "real" session start that doesn't involve the party: the villain's move, a distant consequence, a stranger's death. Sets dread or stakes without exposition.
  - *Scale: session* · *Last used: —*
- **Quiet before the storm** — a deliberately low-stakes scene (meal, festival, downtime beat) placed directly before a major confrontation. Contrast does the work. Pairs naturally with the *catastrophe during peace* item in [[campaign-design-preferences]].
  - *Scale: scene, session* · *Last used: —*
- **Pathetic fallacy** — environment mirrors the emotional beat: weather, light, animal behavior. Cheap to deploy, strong on atmosphere; overuse makes weather a spoiler.
  - *Scale: scene* · *Last used: —*
- **Rule of three** — a motif lands on its third occurrence: third visit to a location, third meeting with an NPC, third time the same phrase is spoken. Plan the first two as setup.
  - *Scale: session, mission, arc* · *Last used: —*
- **Flashback** — play a past event as a live scene: an NPC's memory, a vision at a haunted site, a dream. Players can run other roles in the memory — their own ancestors, the villain's old companions.
  - *Scale: scene, session* · *Last used: —*
- **Flashforward / vision** — a prophetic glimpse of a possible future. Commits the DM to nothing; builds dread and gives players something to steer toward or away from.
  - *Scale: scene, session* · *Last used: —*
- **Frame story** — events arrive through a teller: a bard's tale, trial testimony, an interrogation. The teller's bias and omissions are content — what they leave out matters as much as what they say.
  - *Scale: scene, session* · *Last used: —*
- **Eucatastrophe** — salvation at the darkest moment, when defeat looks certain. Must be seeded (a Chekhov's gun finally pulled, an old debt repaid) — unseeded it collapses into deus ex machina, the failure mode of this device.
  - *Scale: scene, session* · *Last used: —*
- **MacGuffin** — an object that motors the plot; its power matters less than what people will do to hold it. Best when multiple factions want it for incompatible reasons.
  - *Scale: mission, arc* · *Last used: —*
- **Peripeteia (dramatic reversal)** — fortunes flip mid-scene: victory becomes rout, capture becomes opportunity, the hostage becomes the captor. Strongest when the flip comes from something already on the table, not new information.
  - *Scale: scene* · *Last used: —*
- **Poetic justice** — fate rhymes with deed: the villain undone by their own weapon, the cheat ruined by their own forgery. Players feel the world is morally coherent without being lectured.
  - *Scale: session, mission* · *Last used: —*
- **Unreliable narrator (table form)** — lore arrives biased: two NPC accounts of the same event conflict, the history book was written by the winners. The party must decide who to believe — and being wrong has a cost.
  - *Scale: session, mission* · *Last used: —*

## Twists

One-shot reveals. Each needs seeding before it fires — an unseeded twist reads as DM fiat, not payoff.

- **The ally was the villain** — a trusted NPC has been steering the party all along. Strongest when the NPC gave real help; the betrayal must recontextualize, not invalidate, past sessions.
  - *Scale: mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **The rescue was the trap** — the cry for help, the captured friend, the besieged village: bait. Works once; players never fully trust a rescue hook again, which is its own ongoing payoff.
  - *Scale: session, mission* · *Deployed: —*
- **Dead NPC returns** — someone the party saw die comes back: undeath, faked death, twin, resurrection with a price. The *how* must obey established canon — check before deploying.
  - *Scale: session, mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **The villain is right** — the antagonist's goal turns out to be justified; their methods are the evil. Forces a choice between stopping them and finishing what they started.
  - *Scale: mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **The patron's true face** — the quest-giver's real agenda surfaces; the party has been doing someone else's dirty work. The jobs were real, the framing was the lie.
  - *Scale: mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **The MacGuffin is a decoy** — the thing they fought for is fake, or the real prize was something they passed over. Lands best when the genuine article was visible earlier.
  - *Scale: session, mission* · *Deployed: —*
- **The shadow behind the throne** — the obvious antagonist answers to a hidden one. Reframes every prior victory as managed loss. This is an arc twist, not a session twist — deploy near an act boundary.
  - *Scale: arc, campaign* · *Deployed: —*
- **The party caused it** — the current crisis is a delayed consequence of something the party did sessions ago. The strongest version uses a choice they made carelessly, not heroically.
  - *Scale: mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **Identity reveal** — an NPC is someone the party already knows: the masked figure, the new name, the changed face. Requires the original connection to have actually mattered at the table.
  - *Scale: session, mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **The legend was misread** — the prophecy, map, or founding story everyone acted on was mistranslated, incomplete, or propaganda. The true version reassigns roles: savior and destroyer, key and lock.
  - *Scale: arc, campaign* · *Deployed: —*
- **Blood reveal** — a kinship surfaces: the villain is a PC's parent, the orphan is the heir, two enemies share a bloodline. Only fires if the relationship recontextualizes choices already made at the table.
  - *Scale: mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **The enemy within** — the antagonist is one of the party: possession, curse, a doppelganger swap, actions taken in dominated sleep. Handle with the player, not to them — recruit them as co-conspirator before the reveal.
  - *Scale: mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **Dead all along** — someone the party has been interacting with is a ghost, a memory-echo, or undead and unaware. The clues were there: never eats, never crosses the threshold, only certain people see them.
  - *Scale: session, mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **The world is not what it seems** — setting-level reveal: the valley is a demiplane, the city is a purgatory, the "sky" is a ceiling. Arc-scale — deploy at an act boundary, never as a session beat.
  - *Scale: arc, campaign* · *Deployed: —*
- **The monster was the guardian** — the feared creature or "villain" was holding back something worse. Inverse of the ally-was-villain twist; lands hardest after the party has already hurt or killed it.
  - *Scale: mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **False victory** — the triumph itself unleashes the real threat: the seal they broke, the tyrant whose death uncorks the warlords, the cure that spreads the plague. Differs from *the party caused it* by being immediate — the quest was the harm.
  - *Scale: mission, arc* · *Deployed: —*
- **The prophecy caused itself** — every step taken to prevent the foretold doom is what brings it about. The reveal is the mechanism: show the players the loop their own choices closed.
  - *Scale: arc, campaign* · *Deployed: —*
