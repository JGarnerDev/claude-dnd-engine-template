**Habitats:** [[Jungle]] | [[Plains]]

Stocky and barrel-chested, the baboon moves with a loping confidence that belies its size. Its face is naked, weathered leather pulled tight over a heavy brow, eyes pale and calculating beneath. The males carry themselves like petty warlords — shoulders rolling, teeth bared at the edge of a snarl that never quite breaks into one. They are not large animals. That is the first mistake travelers make.

A baboon troop operates on dominance and memory. They remember faces. They communicate grievance in shrieks and posture, and the whole group moves as a single political body — when the alpha decides you are a threat, every set of teeth in the canopy has already received the verdict. Travelers who have made camp near troop territory report waking to find their food stores raided with unsettling precision, their packs methodically emptied, the animals watching from above with something that looks uncomfortably like contempt.

In jungles where the old empires crumbled and left their ruins to the green, baboons have colonized the stonework — nesting in collapsed temples, sleeping in the carved laps of forgotten gods. They treat the remnants of civilization as furniture. Scholars who have ventured into such sites describe finding them stripped of anything soft or portable, the walls streaked with territorial markings, and the baboons themselves watching with the bored authority of creatures who inherited something they did not build and do not value.
